INTERRUPTED TIME SERIES (ITS) ANALYSIS

Multi-Intervention Segmented Regression for Health Programme Evaluation

ABOUT THIS ANALYSIS

What is ITS Analysis?

Interrupted Time Series (ITS) uses segmented regression to evaluate one or more interventions on a repeatedly measured outcome. This tool supports multiple interventions, each producing its own level change (β₂ₖ) and slope change (β₃ₖ) estimate.

When to Use ITS

  • Evaluating malaria policy or LLIN distribution impact
  • Assessing sequential health campaign effects
  • Monitoring reporting rate changes after DHIS2 training
  • Any programme with time-series data and known intervention dates

Requirements & Assumptions

  • Year + Month columns in your data file
  • Enough pre-period data before first intervention
  • Independence: No major confounders coinciding
  • Linearity: Roughly linear trends in each segment
SEGMENTED REGRESSION MODEL

Multi-Intervention ITS Model

Yt = β0 + β1·T + Σk(β2k·Dk + β3k·Pk) + εt

Each intervention k adds a level-change dummy Dk and a time-since-intervention variable Pk.
β2k = immediate level change at intervention k  |  β3k = slope change after intervention k

β0
Baseline Intercept
Estimated outcome at the start of the series (T = 1). Represents the baseline level before any intervention effects.
β1
Pre-Intervention Slope
Monthly rate of change in the outcome before the first intervention. Tells you the underlying trend before any programme began.
β2k
Level Change (Intervention k)
Immediate step change at the time of intervention k. Negative value = sudden drop; positive = sudden increase.
β3k
Slope Change (Intervention k)
Change in the monthly trend after intervention k. Negative = slowing/reversal of an increasing trend; positive = acceleration.
Dk
Intervention Dummy k
Binary: 0 before intervention k, 1 at and after intervention k. Controls the level-change estimate β2k.
Pk
Time Since Intervention k
0 before intervention k; 1, 2, 3, … counting months after. Controls the slope-change estimate β3k.
R²
Model Fit
Proportion of variance explained. Values ≥ 0.70 indicate good fit. Interpret alongside Durbin-Watson.
DW
Durbin-Watson
Tests residual autocorrelation. DW ≈ 2: OK. DW < 1.5: positive autocorrelation. DW > 2.5: negative autocorrelation.

Step 1 — Find β₁ (Pre-Intervention Slope)

We use only the 6 pre-intervention months (T=1 to T=6) to fit a straight line. The formula for the slope of a best-fit line is:

β₁ = Σ[(T − T̄)(Y − Ȳ)] ÷ Σ[(T − T̄)²]
T̄ = average of T values  |  Ȳ = average of Y values  (pre-period only)

First compute the averages of the pre-period:

T̄ = (1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6) ÷ 6 = 21 ÷ 6 = 3.5
Ȳ = (200 + 208 + 216 + 224 + 232 + 240) ÷ 6 = 1320 ÷ 6 = 220
These two averages (T̄ = 3.5, Ȳ = 220) are the only averages used in the entire calculation. Everything else is individual month arithmetic.

Every month before the ITNs arrived, cases were rising by exactly 8 per month. This is β₁. No average was used to get here — only the two means T̄ and Ȳ as intermediate steps in the formula.
Step 2 — Find β₀ (The Starting Point at T=1)

Once we have β₁ = 8, β₀ falls out of one formula:

β₀ = Ȳ − β₁ × T̄
β₀ = 220 − 8 × 3.5
β₀ = 220 − 28
β₀ = 192
The pre-intervention trend line starts at 192 at T=1 (January 2022). This is not an average of anything. It is the point where the best-fit line crosses T=1. You can verify: 192 + 8×1 = 200 (matches January). 192 + 8×6 = 240 (matches June). The line fits perfectly.
Why not just use January's actual value (200) as β₀? Because β₀ is the value of the trend line at T=1, not the raw January observation. If your data has noise, the line may not pass exactly through any single point. In this clean example they happen to match, but in real-world noisy data they will differ slightly.
What is a "Line of Best Fit"? — In Plain Words

Imagine you are a nurse recording the weight of a child every month for 6 months. You write the numbers on a piece of paper:

Jan: 8.0 kg
Feb: 8.3 kg
Mar: 8.5 kg
Apr: 8.9 kg
May: 9.1 kg
Jun: 9.4 kg

You plot these 6 dots on graph paper. They go upward. You want to draw one straight line that comes as close as possible to all 6 dots at the same time. Not touching all of them perfectly — just getting as close as it can to all of them together. That line is the line of best fit.

Line of best fit = the single straight line that is the shortest total distance from all your data points simultaneously.
Another name for it: regression line or trend line.

The calculation finds this line by minimising the sum of the squared gaps between the line and each dot. This method is called Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) — "least" because it minimises, "squares" because it squares the gaps before adding them. Squaring makes large errors matter more than small ones, so the line avoids being pulled off by a single outlier month.

The red line gets as close as possible to all 6 blue dots simultaneously. The yellow gaps are what OLS minimises.

Why Do We Care Where the Line Touches T=1?

The line of best fit extends in both directions infinitely. We ask: "Where does it sit at the very beginning of our data — at T=1?" That answer is β₀.

Think of it like building a road. Before you can say how fast the road rises or falls (β₁, the slope), you need to know the starting altitude — the height at the very beginning of the road. β₀ is that starting altitude.

Reason 1 — It Anchors the Entire Line
A straight line needs two things: a starting point and a slope. β₀ is the starting point. Without it you cannot draw the line at all, because you would not know where on the vertical axis it begins. Once you fix β₀ and β₁, every single monthly prediction (Ŷ) follows automatically.
Reason 2 — It Drives the Counterfactual
The counterfactual — what cases would have been without the ITNs — is computed as β₀ + β₁×T for every month including the intervention month. If β₀ is wrong by even a small amount, every single counterfactual value is shifted, and β₂ (the impact estimate) is wrong by exactly the same amount.
Reason 3 — Meaningful Baseline for Reporting
Programme managers often report: "At the start of the monitoring period, the district had approximately X cases per month." β₀ gives the trend-adjusted version of that statement — more precise than just quoting January's raw number, which may have been unusually high or low due to random fluctuation that month.
Why T=1 and Not T=0?
In many textbooks β₀ is described as "the value when T=0." But T=0 does not exist in your data — your first month is T=1. Technically β₀ is the line's value one month before your data starts. This is why β₀ alone is rarely reported or interpreted — it is a mathematical anchor, not a real observed period. What matters is that it makes β₁, β₂, and β₃ correct.
Simple summary: β₀ on its own is not the interesting number. It is the foundation that holds the entire model upright so that β₁ (pre-trend), β₂ (immediate impact), and β₃ (trend change) are estimated correctly. If you move β₀, every other estimate shifts with it. So the model finds β₀ first — then everything else is measured from that fixed starting point.
Step 3 — The Counterfactual at July 2022 (T=7)

The counterfactual answers: "What would cases have been in July 2022 if the ITN campaign never happened?" We just extend the pre-period trend line one more step forward to T=7:

Counterfactual at T=7 = β₀ + β₁ × 7
Counterfactual = 192 + 8 × 7
Counterfactual = 192 + 56
Counterfactual = 248
Without the ITNs, the model predicts 248 cases in July 2022. This is the dashed red line in your chart at T=7. We never actually observed this — it is a mathematical projection of where the pre-trend was heading.
This is what makes ITS different from a simple before-after comparison. A simple comparison would use the average of the 6 pre-months (220) as the baseline. ITS uses 248 — the trend-adjusted value. Using 220 would incorrectly credit the ITNs with cases that were already rising toward 248 anyway.
Step 4 — Find β₂ (Immediate Level Change at July 2022)

We observed 180 cases in July 2022. The counterfactual said we should have had 248. β₂ is simply the difference:

β₂ = Observed at T=7 − Counterfactual at T=7
β₂ = 180 − 248
β₂ = −68
The ITNs are associated with 68 fewer cases in the very month they were distributed. This is β₂. One subtraction. No average. No complexity. It is the vertical gap between the red dashed line and the actual July observation in your chart.
Timing matters for β₂. If ITNs were distributed on July 1st, all 31 nights of July were spent under nets — β₂ captures the full monthly effect. If distribution happened July 25th, only 6 nights were under nets in July — β₂ will be small, and most of the impact will appear from August onwards in β₃. The model cannot see days — only whole months.
Step 5 — Find β₃ (Change in Monthly Trend After July 2022)

Look at what happened month by month after July:

MonthTCases (Y)Change from previous month

The post-intervention slope is −10 cases per month. The pre-intervention slope was +8 per month (β₁). β₃ is the difference:

β₃ = Post-Intervention Slope − Pre-Intervention Slope (β₁)
β₃ = −10 − (+8)
β₃ = −18
After the ITN campaign, the monthly trend shifted by −18 per month compared to what it was before. Before: rising 8/month. After: falling 10/month. β₃ = −18 captures that entire swing.
Post-slope = β₁ + β₃ = 8 + (−18) = −10. This confirms our arithmetic. The model's post-period trend line falls by 10 cases every month — matching the raw data perfectly in this clean example.

β₀ = 192
Starting value
The trend line sits at 192 at T=1 (January). Computed as Ȳ − β₁×T̄ = 220 − 28. Not January's raw value. Not the pre-period average (220). The intercept of the fitted line at T=1.
β₁ = +8
Pre-ITN monthly rise
Before ITNs, Bo District gained 8 cases every single month. Computed from all 6 pre-months using the slope formula. Uses T̄ and Ȳ as stepping stones — but the result itself is a slope, not an average.
β₂ = −68
Immediate ITN shock
In July itself — the ITN month — cases were 68 lower than the trend predicted (248). One subtraction: 180 − 248 = −68. This is the overnight visible impact. Compared to the trend line, not the average.
β₃ = −18
Monthly trend swing
After ITNs, the monthly direction swung by 18 per month: from +8 (rising) to −10 (falling). β₃ = −10 − (+8) = −18. Every month after July adds another −10 to the case count, on top of the −68 already banked from β₂.
Post-slope = −10
β₁ + β₃ = 8 + (−18)
The actual monthly direction after the ITN campaign. Combines the old pre-trend with the new slope change. Negative means cases are falling. Every 10 months post-ITN = 100 fewer cases per month than at the start of the post-period.
Total at Oct (T=10)
β₂ + β₃×3 = −68 + (−54)
By October (3 months after ITN), the cumulative gap versus counterfactual = −68 + (−18×3) = −68 − 54 = −122 cases. The counterfactual predicted 272. Actual: 150. That 122-case gap is the total protection 3 months in.
PRACTICAL EXAMPLES — HOW TO READ YOUR RESULTS
Who is this for? These five real-world scenarios walk you through what each number in your ITS output actually means — in plain language, without statistical jargon. Each example shows a realistic set of results and explains what a health officer or programme manager should conclude.
01
LLIN Distribution Reduces Malaria Cases
Scenario: Immediate drop + sustained improvement after bed-net campaign
▼
Context: The National Malaria Control Programme distributed long-lasting insecticidal nets (LLINs) to all households in Kenema District in March 2022. You have monthly malaria case counts from January 2020 to December 2023. You run an ITS analysis with one intervention at 2022-03.
Your ITS output shows:
ParameterValuep-valueWhat it means in plain language
β₀ (Intercept)487.2—In January 2020, before any campaign, your district was recording about 487 malaria cases per month as a starting point.
β₁ (Pre-slope)+3.80.041Before the net distribution, malaria was rising by about 4 cases every month. The situation was getting steadily worse — roughly 46 more cases per year just from the trend alone.
β₂ (Level change)−142.0<0.001The moment the LLIN campaign launched, cases dropped by 142 immediately — that same month. This is the "shock" effect: rapid impact that shows the nets worked right away.
β₃ (Slope change)−5.10.003After the campaign, not only did cases drop, but the monthly rate continued falling by an extra 5 cases every month. So the programme did not just give a one-time benefit — it kept improving over time.
Post-slope−1.3—After the intervention, malaria was declining by about 1–2 cases per month. Compare this to pre-campaign when it was rising by 4. The trend completely reversed.
R²0.87—The model explains 87% of all variation in monthly case counts. Very strong — the model fits your data well.
DW1.91—Close to 2, so no autocorrelation problem. Your standard errors and p-values are reliable.
Bottom line for a programme manager: The LLIN campaign was highly effective. Cases fell sharply right after distribution (−142) and kept falling month after month (−5/month). If no action had been taken, the model predicts cases would have reached ~650/month by December 2023. Instead, they were around 280. The counterfactual gap represents roughly 4,400 cases averted over two years.
Key learning — β₁ vs β₃: Think of β₁ as the speed your car was accelerating before you hit the brakes, and β₃ as how hard you braked. β₁ = +3.8 means the car was speeding up. β₃ = −5.1 means you braked harder than the car was accelerating, so you actually reversed direction. The post-slope (β₁ + β₃ = −1.3) tells you the car is now slowing down.
02
New Drug Policy — Trend Change But No Immediate Drop
Scenario: No sudden effect, but the trajectory improves gradually
▼
Context: In July 2021, the Ministry of Health switched the first-line malaria treatment from SP to Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL) in Bo District. You track monthly confirmed malaria deaths from January 2019 to June 2023. You expect change to be gradual — it takes time for facilities to restock, for health workers to be trained, and for patients to receive correct treatment.
Your ITS output shows:

β₀ (Intercept)34.1—At the start of your observation period, the district was recording about 34 malaria deaths per month.
β₁ (Pre-slope)+0.40.210Before the policy change, deaths were creeping up slowly — about 0.4 more deaths each month. This trend is not statistically significant, meaning the pre-period was fairly flat.
β₂ (Level change)−2.10.380There was no significant immediate drop in deaths the month the new drug policy started. This makes sense — hospitals did not instantly receive AL; training took time.
β₃ (Slope change)−1.80.009Even though there was no sudden drop, deaths started declining by about 1.8 every month from July 2021 onwards. Over 24 months, that adds up to roughly 43 fewer deaths.
Post-slope−1.4—After the policy change, the overall monthly trend is −1.4 deaths per month. The pre-period trend was +0.4. Direction flipped from rising to falling.

Bottom line: The drug policy worked — but slowly. You would not see a sharp cliff-edge drop in your chart. Instead you would see a gradual downward slope after July 2021 that gets steeper over time. β₂ not significant is expected here — it just means "no overnight miracle." The significant β₃ is the real story: the new treatment protocol steadily reduced mortality month after month.
Key learning — when β₂ is not significant: A non-significant level change does not mean the intervention failed. It simply means there was no abrupt jump. For policy changes that take time to implement (training, supply chain, behaviour change), a significant β₃ with a non-significant β₂ is actually the expected and correct pattern. Look at both together before drawing conclusions.
03
DHIS2 Training — Reporting Rate Change, Not Disease Change
Scenario: Cases appear to rise after intervention — but it is improved reporting, not a real increase
▼
Context: In April 2022, all health facilities in Kono District received intensive DHIS2 data entry training. You track monthly facility reporting rates (% of expected reports submitted). Before training, many facilities were not submitting monthly reports at all. After training, nearly all do.
Your ITS output shows:
ParameterValuep-valuePlain-language meaning
β₀ (Intercept)51.3%—At the start of your data, only about 51% of facilities were submitting reports on time each month.
β₁ (Pre-slope)+0.2%0.540Before training, reporting was barely improving — just 0.2 percentage points per month, which is not statistically meaningful. The system was essentially stuck.
β₂ (Level change)+28.4%<0.001The month after training, reporting rates jumped by 28 percentage points immediately — from around 53% to 81% overnight. Training had an instant, dramatic effect.
β₃ (Slope change)+1.1%0.022After the initial jump, reporting continued improving by about 1 percentage point each month. Health workers kept getting better at submitting data as they practiced.

Bottom line: The training was a complete success for system performance. Both β₂ and β₃ are positive and significant — an immediate large jump followed by continued steady improvement. Important caveat: You may also notice that recorded malaria case counts went up after training. Do not interpret this as worsening malaria. It reflects better reporting — previously hidden cases are now being captured. Always consider context when interpreting ITS on case count data after system-strengthening activities.
Key learning — positive β₂ and β₃ are not always bad: For disease outcomes, a positive β₂ means cases went up — usually bad. But for process indicators like reporting rate, coverage %, or bed occupancy, positive means improvement. Always read the sign of β₂ and β₃ in context of what you are measuring, not just whether it is positive or negative.
04
Two Sequential Interventions — Measuring Additive Impact
Scenario: LLIN campaign followed by IPTp scale-up — did the second intervention add value beyond the first?
▼
Context: Bombali District ran an LLIN mass campaign in January 2022, then scaled up Intermittent Preventive Treatment in pregnancy (IPTp) in September 2023. You track monthly malaria cases in children under-5 from January 2020 to December 2024. You run ITS with two interventions.
Your ITS output shows:
ParameterValuep-valuePlain-language meaning
β₀ (Intercept)621.0—Starting point: about 621 under-5 malaria cases per month at the beginning of 2020.
β₁ (Pre-slope)+5.20.018Before any intervention, cases were growing by about 5 per month — a significant upward trend. The burden was increasing.
β₂₋₁ (LLIN — Level)−188.0<0.001The LLIN campaign caused an immediate drop of 188 cases in January 2022. Very large and highly significant — the nets had rapid impact on children's malaria burden.
β₃₋₁ (LLIN — Slope)−4.10.007After the LLIN campaign, cases continued falling by about 4 per month. The nets kept working, and coverage may have continued improving.
β₂₋₂ (IPTp — Level)−67.00.031When IPTp was scaled up in Sept 2023, cases dropped by an additional 67 that same month — on top of everything the LLIN campaign had already achieved. IPTp added its own independent benefit.
β₃₋₂ (IPTp — Slope)−1.20.210IPTp's additional slope change was not statistically significant. The main benefit from IPTp was the immediate level drop, not a further acceleration of the declining trend.
R²0.91—Excellent fit — the two-intervention model explains 91% of variation.
DW1.84—Acceptable. No autocorrelation concern.
Bottom line: Both interventions worked. The LLIN campaign had the bigger impact (−188 immediately, −4/month ongoing). IPTp then added an extra −67 cases on top. The ITS model cleanly separates the contribution of each programme — something a simple before-after comparison cannot do. This is the key advantage of multi-intervention ITS: you can present evidence for each programme's independent value to funders, even when programmes overlap in time.
Key learning — separating programme effects: Without ITS, a donor might ask "how do we know which programme reduced cases?" With two-intervention ITS, β₂₋₁ and β₂₋₂ give separate, independent answers. β₂₋₂ = −67 means: even after accounting for everything the LLIN campaign did, IPTp still reduced cases by 67 more. This is called the marginal impact of the second intervention, and it is a very powerful number for programme justification.
05
Intervention Shows No Effect — What This Means and What To Do
Scenario: Neither β₂ nor β₃ is significant — how to interpret and respond
▼
Context: In June 2022, the district health team ran a social and behaviour change communication (SBCC) radio campaign encouraging people to sleep under nets and seek early treatment. You track monthly confirmed malaria cases from January 2021 to December 2023. The campaign ran for 3 months. You expect modest effects.
Your ITS output shows:
ParameterValuep-valuePlain-language meaning
β₀ (Intercept)398.0—Starting baseline of about 398 confirmed cases per month.
β₁ (Pre-slope)−1.20.290Before the campaign, cases were very slightly declining, but not significantly — essentially flat. No dramatic pre-existing trend to confuse the results.
β₂ (Level change)−8.50.412Cases dropped by about 8 the month of the campaign, but this could easily be random variation. The p-value of 0.41 is far above 0.05 — we cannot conclude it was the campaign.
β₃ (Slope change)−0.60.580The trend barely changed. A drop of 0.6 per month with p = 0.58 is statistical noise, not a real trend shift.
R²0.41—The model only explains 41% of variation — poor fit. Other factors (seasonality, rainfall) are probably driving the data more than the intervention.
DW1.38—Below 1.5 — positive autocorrelation detected. This means consecutive months are correlated, standard errors may be underestimated, and some p-values could be artificially small. Interpret with extra caution.
Bottom line — no detectable effect: The ITS analysis finds no statistically significant impact of the radio campaign on malaria cases. This does not necessarily mean the campaign was useless — it may mean: (1) the effect was too small to detect with this sample size; (2) the 3-month campaign was too short; (3) cases are driven more by seasonality than behaviour; or (4) the outcome (confirmed cases) is the wrong metric — care-seeking behaviour change may not appear in case counts for months. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.
Key learning — what to do when results are not significant:
  • Check DW first: DW = 1.38 here means autocorrelation is a problem. Re-run with Newey-West corrected standard errors or Prais-Winsten regression for more reliable inference.
  • Add seasonality controls: Malaria is strongly seasonal. If you have not controlled for rainy season effects, the model may be missing a key driver. Add sine/cosine seasonal terms or use seasonal dummies.
  • Consider a longer follow-up: Behaviour change campaigns often need 6–12 months to show measurable impact in case data. If you only have 6 months post-intervention, you may have insufficient power.
  • Try a more proximal outcome: Instead of confirmed cases, measure care-seeking rates, proportion treated within 24 hours, or net usage surveys — outcomes that respond faster to SBCC.
  • Report honestly: "No significant effect detected" is a valid, important finding. Publish it. It tells the programme community what does not work under these conditions.
